#+title: Number Sets

The set of numbers used in mathematics is divided into several overlapping **subsets**, each with unique properties. Understanding these sets is fundamental to number theory and algebra.

* **Hierarchy of Number Sets:**

\[
\mathbb{W} \subset \mathbb{Z} \subset \mathbb{Q} \subset \mathbb{R}
\]

- \( \mathbb{W} \): [[id:c8e9f940-8d1f-434a-a27f-9668abde0be0][Whole Numbers]]
- \( \mathbb{Z} \): [[id:6b3ac140-da38-4146-b559-c0fa52f71e63][Integers]]
- \( \mathbb{Q} \): [[id:f61bc0c5-d455-445c-bda2-c34eda3a7f7d][Rational numbers]]
- \( \mathbb{R} \): [[id:a8e958d9-8b2f-424e-ada1-c432e7f10c47][Real Numbers]]
- \( \mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q} \): [[id:be710910-126e-442c-a6f8-c8760e139e2c][Irattional Numbers]]

* **Set Definitions:**

- **Whole Numbers \( \mathbb{W} \)**:
  \[
  \{0, 1, 2, 3, \dots\}
  \]

- **Integers \( \mathbb{Z} \)**:
  \[
  \{\dots, -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, \dots\}
  \]

- **Rational Numbers \( \mathbb{Q} \)**:
  Numbers that can be expressed as a fraction of two integers: \( \frac{a}{b} \), with \( b \ne 0 \)

- **Irrational Numbers**:
  Numbers that **cannot** be written as fractions. Their decimal representations are **non-repeating, non-terminating**.
  Examples: \( \pi, \sqrt{2}, e \)

- **Real Numbers \( \mathbb{R} \)**:
  Includes **all** rational and irrational numbers. Any number on the **number line**.
